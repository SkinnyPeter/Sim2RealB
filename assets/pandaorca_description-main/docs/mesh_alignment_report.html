2. Approach

The alignment script (scripts/compute_alignment.py) extracts geometric features directly from the STL meshes to compute rigid-body transforms:

  1. Face clustering — group triangles by normal direction (5° tolerance, area-weighted averaging) to find dominant flat surfaces.
  2. Boundary-loop extraction — for each face cluster, find edges shared by only one triangle (boundary edges), then trace closed loops.
  3. Circular-hole detection — filter loops by radius (2–6 mm), circularity (std/mean < 0.15), and vertex count (≥ 12) to identify bolt holes.
  4. Orthonormal frame construction — from a pair of hole centres + face normal, build a right-handed frame [u, v, n].
  5. Mating transform — align source frame to destination frame with anti-parallel normals (face-to-face contact) via R = F_dst_flipped · F_src−1.
  6. Disambiguation — resolve 180° flip and 4-fold bolt-pattern symmetry by (a) checking connector centroid in +Z from link8 and (b) matching the orca-face bend direction to the existing URDF.

5. Interface 1 — link8 → connector_mount

Method

The Panda link8 frame is at the flange face: bolt holes should lie in the XY-plane at Z = 0 with radial distance 25 mm from the origin. We iterate over all permutations of panda-face holes and both flip modes to find the transform that maps them onto this pattern.

Disambiguation

  • Physical check: connector centroid must be at Z > 0 in link8 (bracket extends “above” the flange).
  • Angular match: among candidates with connector centroid in +Z, select the one whose orca-face normal direction best matches the existing URDF’s orientation. This resolves the 4-fold bolt-pattern symmetry (design choice of which direction the bracket bends from the flange).

6b. Right Hand — connector_mount → orcahand_world

Key insight: mirroring is not a rotation. The left and right OrcaHand URDFs are mirror images of each other — the right hand’s world frame has its X-axis pointing opposite to the left’s. This is an improper transformation (det = −1) that cannot be represented as a URDF rotation. Therefore, the left hand’s connector_mount → world transform cannot be reused for the right hand, even with sign flips on individual components. The alignment must be recomputed from the right hand’s own tower-camera mesh.

7. Known Limitation — Tower Mesh Penetration

Visual overlap at the connector-orca interface. The OrcaHand tower-camera mesh extends 57 mm above its mounting face (in the +Z direction) but only 4 mm below. Because the mate transform maps the mounting face normal (+Z) to −nOC (anti-parallel, for face-to-face contact), the tower body (+Z) extends in the −nOC direction — which passes through the 6 mm thick connector wall into the bracket’s concave space.

ResultTower body passes through the 6 mm wall

Why a simple offset doesn’t fix it

The tower body extends 57 mm along a direction that is almost exactly anti-parallel to the connector face normal. A translational offset along nOC shifts the entire orca assembly but cannot change the 57 mm of body that passes through the wall. Both +nOC and −nOC offsets were tested (3–8 mm); neither significantly reduced penetration.

Why a rotation fix flips the hand to the wrong side

Negating the mounting-face normal (using −Z instead of +Z) correctly places the tower body on the outside of the connector. However, this also flips the entire orca assembly — the fingers and palm end up on the opposite side of the bracket, which is physically incorrect.

Root cause

The tower-camera mesh serves double duty: it is both the mounting interface (bolt holes at the base, z ≈ 0) and a tall structural tower extending upward. In the assembled configuration the hand’s fingers correctly extend from the bracket, but the tower structure necessarily passes through the connector wall. This is a geometric property of the mesh and the 90° bracket configuration, not an alignment error.

Recommendation: If the visual overlap is undesirable for rendering, consider (a) splitting the tower-camera mesh into separate mounting-plate and tower-body parts, or (b) using a clipping plane in the visualizer, or (c) accepting the overlap as a cosmetic artifact of the URDF representation.

9. Symmetry & Ambiguity Notes

Sources of ambiguity
TypeDescriptionResolution
180° flip Two ways to mate faces (A/B flip modes) Connector centroid must be in +Z from link8; orca body must extend outward
4-fold bolt symmetry Square bolt pattern → 4 valid rotations Match orca-face bend direction to existing URDF orientation
Hole pairing (orca) 2 orca holes vs 6 possible pairs from 4 connector holes Select pair with spacing closest to orca hole spacing (47.94 vs 50.00 mm)
Left/right mirroring Left and right hand URDFs use mirrored coordinate frames (X-axis flipped). This is an improper transformation (det = −1) that cannot be expressed as a URDF rotation. Recompute the connector → world transform from scratch using the correct hand’s tower-camera mesh and world-to-tower offset. Do not attempt to derive the right hand transform by sign-flipping components of the left hand transform.

10. Script Reference

All computations are in scripts/compute_alignment.py. The script supports both left and right hand configurations via the --hand flag:

cd pandaorca_description && python3 scripts/compute_alignment.py --hand right
